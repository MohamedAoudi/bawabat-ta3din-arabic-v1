# AMIP Country Profiles

These country profiles describe the solid-minerals context for the 21 Arab countries covered by the Arab Mining Indicators Portal. Production coverage spans 2010-2024, while trade coverage currently runs through 2023.

## Algeria

Algeria has a broad solid-minerals base that includes iron ore, phosphate rock, barite, bentonite, kaolin, limestone, gypsum, salt, marble, aggregates, and cement raw materials. Its mineral geography combines Saharan deposits, northern industrial markets, and long transport corridors linking mines, plants, and ports. Iron and phosphate are important strategic minerals, while industrial minerals support construction, ceramics, drilling services, fertilizer production, and local manufacturing. Algeria's mining sector is significant because it can diversify industrial activity, supply domestic infrastructure, and support regional trade in selected minerals. In AMIP, Algeria should be described as a diversified solid-minerals producer, not as a single-commodity country. The most visible AMIP commodities include iron ore, phosphate rock, cement, gypsum, salt, barite, bentonite, marble, pozzolan, silica sand, zinc products, and aggregates.

## Bahrain

Bahrain has limited mine geology and a small land area, so its AMIP profile is centered on downstream mineral processing, industrial trade, and construction materials rather than large-scale extraction. The country is notable for primary aluminium and ferro-alloy activity, while local quarrying is limited compared with larger Arab producers. Bahrain imports many raw or semi-processed mineral inputs and exports selected manufactured or processed mineral products through its industrial and port infrastructure. Its solid-minerals relevance therefore comes from value addition, metal processing, logistics, and trade participation. AMIP answers should be careful not to invent a broad mining sector for Bahrain. The most relevant commodities include primary aluminium, steel-related products, ferro-alloys, construction materials, cement inputs, and mineral trade products.

## Djibouti

Djibouti has a limited but distinctive solid-minerals profile. Salt is the best-known mineral commodity, linked to arid basins and evaporitic environments. Construction materials such as aggregates, sand, and cement inputs also matter for domestic infrastructure. The country's wider mineral role is shaped by port logistics, regional trade routes, and proximity to Red Sea and Horn of Africa markets rather than by a large diversified mining industry. AMIP responses should describe Djibouti honestly as a country with selective mineral activity and important logistics functions, not as a major metallic-mining producer. Mineral-sector opportunities depend on mapping, permitting, transport, and responsible development of local resources. The main AMIP-relevant commodities are salt, cement, aggregates, construction materials, and traded mineral products.

## Egypt

Egypt is one of the Arab region's most diversified solid-minerals countries. Its profile includes gold, phosphate rock, iron ore, ilmenite, manganese, kaolin, feldspar, quartz, silica sand, gypsum, limestone, cement, salt, talc, vermiculite, tin, and dimension stone. The Eastern Desert, Sinai, Red Sea coast, Western Desert, and Nile Valley industrial belt each contribute different mineral settings and processing opportunities. Egypt's mining sector supports fertilizer production, steel, cement, ceramics, glass, building materials, and precious-metal activity. It is also a major mineral trade and manufacturing market, so AMIP answers should connect mine production with domestic processing and imports. The most visible commodities include gold, phosphate rock, iron and steel products, cement, gypsum, salt, feldspar, kaolin, silica sand, manganese, and ilmenite.

## Iraq

Iraq's solid-minerals profile includes phosphate rock, sulfur, salt, gypsum, limestone, cement materials, bauxite occurrences, kaolin, clay, sand, and construction raw materials. Some resources are associated with western and northern sedimentary basins, evaporite sequences, and industrial-mineral formations. The sector has important potential for fertilizers, cement, construction materials, and selected industrial minerals, although production and investment have been affected by infrastructure, rehabilitation, and institutional conditions. AMIP answers should avoid exaggerating current mining breadth while recognizing Iraq's established mineral resources and industrial base. The most relevant AMIP commodities are phosphate rock, sulfur, salt, gypsum, limestone, cement, kaolin, clay, bauxite, silica sand, and sand.

## Jordan

Jordan is a major Arab solid-minerals country with a strong focus on phosphate rock, potash, bromine, Dead Sea mineral products, limestone, calcium carbonate, silica sand, basalt, granite, marble, travertine, clay, and cement materials. Its mining economy is closely connected to fertilizer production, chemical processing, construction materials, and exports through regional logistics corridors. Phosphate and potash are the central strategic commodities, while bromine and Dead Sea mud add specialty-mineral and chemical dimensions. Jordan also has important quarrying and industrial-mineral activity serving glass, ceramics, cement, and building materials. In AMIP, Jordan should be described as a concentrated but sophisticated producer with strong downstream links. Key commodities include phosphate rock, potash, bromine, Dead Sea mud, silica sand, limestone, basalt, marble, travertine, and cement inputs.

## Kuwait

Kuwait has a limited solid-minerals extraction profile, with activity focused mainly on construction materials, lime, sand, gravel, cement inputs, and selected industrial processing or trade. Its geology and land-use context do not support the same breadth of mining found in larger Arab countries, so AMIP answers should be cautious and factual. Kuwait's mineral relevance is often linked to infrastructure demand, imports of raw and processed mineral products, and downstream consumption by construction and industry. Local quarrying and mineral-material supply are important for domestic projects, but the country should not be described as a major metallic-mining producer. AMIP-relevant commodities include lime, sulfur products where recorded, steel trade and processing, aggregates, cement materials, and imported mineral goods.

## Lebanon

Lebanon's solid-minerals sector is concentrated in quarrying and construction materials. Limestone, gypsum, cement raw materials, marble, aggregates, sand, salt, and lime are the most relevant AMIP commodities. The country has a long building-stone and cement tradition, supported by carbonate geology and domestic construction demand. Mining activity is limited compared with countries that produce large metallic or fertilizer minerals, and environmental management, land-use pressure, and permitting are important issues for quarry operations. AMIP answers should present Lebanon as a construction-material and industrial-mineral country rather than a major metals producer. Its mineral economy matters for cement, building products, local infrastructure, and trade in processed or semi-processed materials.

## Libya

Libya's solid-minerals profile includes iron ore resources, gypsum, limestone, salt, silica, clay, cement materials, construction aggregates, and selected industrial minerals. Iron ore is the best-known strategic mineral opportunity, while carbonate and evaporite resources support construction and industrial uses. The sector has long-term geological potential, but current mining activity is more limited and uneven than in several other Arab countries. AMIP answers should describe Libya as a country with important mineral resources and selected production, while avoiding unsupported claims of broad active mining. Its mineral significance lies in iron and steel potential, cement raw materials, gypsum, salt, quarry products, and future industrial diversification. Relevant AMIP commodities include iron ore, pig iron or steel products where recorded, gypsum, limestone, cement, sulfur products, and salt.

## Mauritania

Mauritania is one of the Arab region's most mining-centered economies. Iron ore is the flagship commodity, and gold and copper are also important. The country also appears in AMIP data for cement, gypsum, salt, and steel-related products. Mauritania's mineral geography connects inland deposits with rail, port, and export logistics, making transport infrastructure central to the mining economy. Mining supports public revenue, employment, service industries, and regional development, but AMIP documents should not attach unsupported figures to those roles. The country is best described as a major Arab producer of iron ore with additional precious- and base-metal production. Key commodities include iron ore, gold, copper, gypsum, salt, cement, and steel-related products.

## Morocco

Morocco is among the world's leading phosphate countries and has one of the Arab region's most diversified solid-minerals portfolios. Beyond phosphate rock and fertilizer products, it produces or processes cobalt, lead, zinc, copper, silver, gold, barite, fluorspar, manganese, bentonite, feldspar, salt, gypsum, talc, limestone, and building stone. Mining is linked to chemical processing, metallurgy, ports, research, and export-oriented industrial policy. Morocco's importance in AMIP comes from both scale and diversity: it combines globally significant fertilizer value chains with polymetallic districts and industrial minerals. AMIP answers should use qualitative language such as "leading" or "major" rather than unsupported quantities. Key commodities include phosphate rock, fertilizers, cobalt, zinc, lead, copper, silver, gold, barite, fluorspar, salt, and industrial minerals.

## Oman

Oman has a diversified solid-minerals sector based on gypsum, limestone, chromite, copper, marble, clay, kaolin, quartz, silica, aggregates, building materials, and selected downstream metals. Its port access and proximity to Asian and regional markets strengthen the export potential of bulk industrial minerals, especially where quarry operations are close to logistics infrastructure. Oman's mining history includes copper and chromite, while modern activity is strongly associated with gypsum, limestone, construction materials, and industrial-mineral development. AMIP answers should describe Oman as an active quarrying and industrial-minerals producer with selected metallic-mineral activity. Key commodities include gypsum, limestone, chromite, copper, marble, silica, quartz, clay, kaolin, primary aluminium, ferrochromium, and construction materials.

## Palestine

Palestine has a limited solid-minerals sector dominated by stone, marble, limestone, aggregates, and construction materials. Quarrying and stone cutting are economically important, especially for local building products and exportable finished stone, but the country does not have a broad large-scale metallic-mining industry in AMIP. Access, land constraints, permitting, and movement restrictions shape mineral activity and trade. AMIP answers should be honest about the limited scope while recognizing the significance of stone and quarry industries for employment, small businesses, and construction supply. The most relevant commodities are limestone, marble and stone products, aggregates, sand, cement inputs, and imported mineral goods. Palestine should not be described as an oil, gas, or major metals producer in this corpus.

## Qatar

Qatar's AMIP solid-minerals profile is limited in upstream mining and stronger in downstream processing, trade, and construction-material demand. Local resources include limestone, sand, and cement inputs, while industrial activity includes primary aluminium and mineral-related manufactured products. The country imports many raw and semi-processed mineral materials for construction and industry, and its logistics infrastructure supports trade flows. Qatar should not be described as a broad mining country; its role is better framed around selective quarry materials, mineral-processing industries, and trade participation. AMIP-relevant commodities include limestone, sand, cement raw materials, primary aluminium, steel products, sulfur products where recorded, ammonia, and mineral trade products.

## Saudi Arabia

Saudi Arabia has one of the Arab region's broadest solid-minerals sectors. Its AMIP profile includes phosphate rock, gold, copper, zinc, bauxite, alumina, primary aluminium, magnesite, kaolin, silica sand, quartz, feldspar, limestone, gypsum, salt, basalt, granite, clay, dolomite, barite, diatomite, pyrophyllite, talc, manganese, and steel-chain products. Mining supports fertilizer production, aluminium value chains, precious and base metals, cement, ceramics, glass, and construction materials. The sector is strategically significant for industrial diversification and domestic manufacturing. AMIP answers should emphasize the breadth of solid minerals and downstream processing without introducing unsupported figures. Key commodities include phosphate rock, gold, copper, zinc, bauxite, aluminium, magnesite, silica sand, gypsum, limestone, cement, steel, and industrial minerals.

## Somalia

Somalia has a limited and underdeveloped AMIP production profile, with recorded or potential relevance in industrial minerals and construction materials. Source references include barytes, alumina-content material, cement-related commodities, salt, limestone, gypsum, and quarry materials. The wider geological setting may host additional minerals, but AMIP answers should not speculate beyond documented solid-mineral information. Mining activity is constrained by institutional capacity, infrastructure, security, and data availability, so the country should be described carefully as having selective mineral resources rather than a mature diversified mining sector. Its mineral relevance is local supply, future geological assessment, and regional trade potential. AMIP-relevant commodities include salt, gypsum, limestone, barytes, cement materials, aggregates, and other construction minerals.

## Sudan

Sudan is an important Arab gold producer and also appears in AMIP data for chromite, copper, manganese, gypsum, salt, feldspar, kaolin, marble, mica, talc, silver, iron, cement, and steel-related products. Mining ranges from artisanal and small-scale activity to more formal operations, so governance, traceability, environmental management, and processing capacity are important themes. Gold is the most visible commodity, but Sudan's solid-minerals base is broader than precious metals alone. AMIP answers should use qualitative descriptions and avoid unsupported production or export values. The country's mineral sector matters for livelihoods, public revenue, local industry, and regional trade. Key commodities include gold, chromite, copper, manganese, gypsum, salt, marble, talc, mica, kaolin, feldspar, silver, and cement.

## Syria

Syria's solid-minerals profile includes phosphate rock, gypsum, salt, limestone, basalt, cement raw materials, marble, clay, and other construction minerals. Phosphate and cement inputs are the most visible strategic commodities, while gypsum, salt, and quarry materials support domestic industry and construction. Conflict, infrastructure damage, and institutional disruption have affected mining activity and data continuity, so AMIP answers should be careful, factual, and avoid overstating current capacity. The country has established mineral resources and historical production, but the practical mining picture depends on operating conditions and rehabilitation. AMIP-relevant commodities include phosphate rock, gypsum, salt, limestone, cement, basalt, clay, marble, and building materials.

## Tunisia

Tunisia has a well-established solid-minerals profile centered on phosphate rock and downstream fertilizer activity, supported by salt, gypsum, limestone, cement raw materials, barite, iron ore, lead, zinc, and industrial minerals. Phosphate mining and chemical processing are central to the country's mineral identity, while quarrying and industrial minerals support construction, ceramics, and domestic manufacturing. Tunisia also participates in mineral trade through imports of industrial inputs and exports of selected mineral products. AMIP answers should describe Tunisia as a significant phosphate and industrial-minerals producer without adding unsupported quantities. Key commodities include phosphate rock, fertilizers, salt, gypsum, cement, barite, iron ore, lead, zinc, limestone, and construction materials.

## UAE

The UAE's solid-minerals profile combines quarrying, downstream processing, re-export trade, and industrial manufacturing. It is known in AMIP contexts for aggregates, limestone, gypsum, cement inputs, chromite or chromium records, primary aluminium, refined lead records, steel products, sulfur products, and mineral trade. Quarrying in the northern emirates supplies construction and infrastructure markets, while ports and industrial zones support regional and global mineral flows. The UAE should be described as a major mineral-processing and trade hub with important quarrying activity, rather than as a broad upstream mining country. Key AMIP commodities include aggregates, limestone, gypsum, cement materials, primary aluminium, steel, lead products, chromite-related records, and traded critical minerals.

## Yemen

Yemen's solid-minerals profile includes gypsum, limestone, salt, marble, granite, basalt, volcanic tuff, pozzolan, sand and gravel, cement materials, and potential metallic minerals such as gold, copper, zinc, lead, and silver. AMIP production records emphasize construction and industrial minerals more than large active metal production. The country has varied geology, but mining activity has been constrained by infrastructure, security, investment conditions, and data limitations. AMIP answers should recognize the resource base while avoiding speculative claims about current output. Solid minerals matter for local construction, cement, small quarries, and possible future development when conditions allow. Relevant commodities include gypsum, limestone, salt, marble, granite, basalt, pozzolan, volcanic tuff, sand and gravel, and cement materials.
